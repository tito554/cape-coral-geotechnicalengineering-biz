With over 400 miles of navigable canals carved into a limestone platform, Cape Coral sits on one of Florida's most complex karst landscapes. The city's average elevation barely reaches 5 feet above sea level, and the underlying Ocala Limestone is riddled with dissolution features that can't be seen from the surface. When a developer or engineer needs to know what's between the ground and the competent rock 30 to 80 feet down, seismic tomography provides a continuous velocity model that drilling alone cannot offer. Our field crew deploys 24-channel and 48-channel arrays with geophone spacing adapted to the target depth, acquiring high-fold refraction data along transects that cross suspected voids or paleochannels. The raw shot gathers are processed through iterative ray-tracing inversion algorithms, generating 2D P-wave velocity sections that highlight low-velocity anomalies. For deeper targets where bedrock topography is irregular, we complement the refraction line with a short MASW profile to extract shear-wave velocities and constrain the dynamic soil properties required by ASCE 7 Chapter 20 site classification.

A P-wave velocity below 1,200 m/s in Cape Coral limestone almost always correlates with a dissolution feature — and it's something you won't catch with borings alone.

Scope of work in Cape Coral

A common mistake in Cape Coral is relying solely on SPT borings spaced 50 or 100 feet apart to characterize a site, then being surprised when a sinkhole opens during excavation or dewatering. Limestone dissolution follows preferential flow paths along fractures, and a boring that hits solid rock at 40 feet tells you nothing about a dissolution pipe 15 feet away. Seismic tomography fills that gap by imaging the subsurface laterally. Our standard field protocol for karst investigation uses a 5-foot geophone spacing with sledgehammer and weight-drop sources, acquiring overlapping shot points to ensure ray coverage down to 100 feet. The tomographic inversion produces a velocity model where sound limestone registers above 4,000 m/s, weathered rock falls between 2,000 and 3,500 m/s, and loose sand or air-filled voids drop below 1,200 m/s. For projects near the Caloosahatchee River or in the Yucca Pens area, where the water table fluctuates seasonally, we often run a parallel resistivity line because the electrical contrast between saturated clay and dry sand adds a complementary constraint to the seismic interpretation. The final deliverables include ray-coverage diagrams, RMS error statistics, and depth slices in CAD format ready for integration with the geotechnical model.

Typical technical challenges in Cape Coral

Chapter 18 of the Florida Building Code, which incorporates ASCE 7-22 by reference, requires site-specific ground motion analysis for structures on Site Class E or F soils — and much of Cape Coral falls into these categories due to the prevalence of soft limestone and potential for liquefaction in saturated sands. The 2024 update to the Florida Geological Survey's sinkhole vulnerability map placed western Cape Coral in a zone of elevated dissolution risk, reflecting the density of paleosinkholes mapped from historical aerial photography. When a seismic tomography survey reveals a low-velocity anomaly beneath a proposed footing, the design team must either relocate the load, deepen the foundation to competent bearing, or design a grouting program. We've seen cases where a single tomography line crossing a planned retention pond identified a buried collapse feature that would have compromised the liner and drained the pond into the aquifer. For critical facilities, combining seismic tomography with liquefaction assessment using SPT-based triggering analysis gives the structural engineer a complete picture of both the static and seismic performance of the foundation soils.

Our services

Every seismic survey in Cape Coral is designed around the specific question the client needs answered. These are the four most common configurations we deploy in southwest Florida karst environments:

Karst Void Detection Survey

High-resolution refraction lines with 2.5 ft geophone spacing, optimized for imaging dissolution features between 10 and 60 ft depth. Includes velocity anomaly mapping with color-coded risk zones.

Bedrock Topography Mapping

Longer refraction spreads with 10 ft spacing, using a weight-drop source to reach depths of 100+ ft. Delivers a continuous bedrock surface profile for foundation design.

Seismic Reflection Profiling

CDP reflection acquisition for sites where the water table creates a strong acoustic impedance contrast. Useful for mapping stratigraphy below 80 ft where refraction energy is attenuated.

Crosshole Seismic Tomography

Borehole-to-borehole P-wave and S-wave velocity imaging between existing borings. Achieves resolution down to 1 ft and is ideal for confirming the geometry of grouted zones or compaction treatment.

Frequently asked questions

How deep can seismic refraction see in Cape Coral's limestone?

Typical penetration with a sledgehammer source and a 115-foot spread is around 30 to 40 feet. Using a weight drop or accelerated weight drop, we can reach 80 to 120 feet reliably. The actual depth depends on the velocity contrast between the overburden and the limestone — in Cape Coral, where the sand and weathered rock overlying the Ocala Limestone can be 40 to 60 feet thick, a 48-channel spread with 10-foot geophone spacing typically images to 100 feet with good ray coverage.

What does a seismic tomography survey cost for a standard residential lot in Cape Coral?

For a typical residential investigation with two to three refraction lines totaling around 300 linear feet, the cost ranges from US$3,090 to US$5,660 depending on the number of shots, the geophone spacing, and whether reflection acquisition is added. The price includes field mobilization, data processing, tomographic inversion, and a signed engineering report with CAD-ready depth sections.

Can seismic methods tell the difference between an air-filled void and a clay-filled sinkhole?

They can, to a useful degree. Air-filled voids produce a strong P-wave velocity drop — often below 1,000 m/s — while clay-filled dissolution features typically range between 1,200 and 1,800 m/s. The tomographic inversion models this as a gradational velocity field rather than a sharp boundary, so the shape of the anomaly combined with the velocity range gives a reliable indication of the fill material. We often recommend a targeted boring or a CPT sounding through the anomaly to confirm the interpretation.

How long does a seismic survey take and will it damage landscaping?

A standard two-line survey on a residential lot takes one field day, with processing and reporting completed within five to seven business days. The geophones are planted with small spikes that leave holes about the diameter of a pencil, and the source impacts are on the ground surface — no drilling, no excavation. We use rubber-matted weight drops near pavers or decorative concrete to avoid any marking. The only disruption is some noise during the shot sequence, which we schedule to minimize disturbance to neighbors.
